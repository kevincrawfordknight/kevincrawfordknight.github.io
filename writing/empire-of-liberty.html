Empire of Liberty

Kevin Knight

1. Scraps of cellophane tape

Peking, 1972.

Alicent Technologies.

That morning, a crowd of software engineers gathered around a new spreadsheet taped to the Wall of 360-Degree Feedbacks. Before the re-organization, they’d called it the Gratitude Wall, but those days were over. "Bombard the Headquarters!" was the new directive from our company’s Chairman and CEO, and now anyone could denounce anyone: just put up a poster, or an index card, and name names. I pushed into the crowd, eager to see who’d been called out and for what, straining to read the small characters that had been line-printed onto the green-and-white paper. A co-worker impatiently elbowed past, shoving me back.

I finally reached the front of the crowd and read the first line of the spreadsheet, which concerned a Level-D2 engineer named Robert Lin who was accused of “dragging his rear” throughout Q4, which made me feel bad for Robert Lin’s teammates. I scanned the next few lines, and my cheeks flushed -- there in black and white (and green) was the last name I expected to see: Vivian Wu.

My name.

 

Name, National ID #, Gender, Age, Weight, Notes.

Vivian Wu, 0001688816681, Female, 19, 59kg, Uncontrolled workplace violence.

 

I felt the judgment of my co-workers crush in on me even as they physically edged away. Something started boiling in my gut, and I tried to take a deep breath. Check your mood ring, I told myself, glancing down. Dark orange: you are distressed, you feel overwhelmed. Looking back up, I saw that my name was still there. Perforated feed-holes lined the edges of the printer paper. I counted them:

Twenty-seven holes on the left side.

Twenty-seven holes on the right side.

Then I lost my shit, as Brenda would say. I ripped the spreadsheet down with both hands, leaving behind only a few scraps of cellophane tape.

2. That’s the whole joke

The name of my company is Alicent Technologies.

How I came to work there is a story that began nineteen years earlier, when I was born the elder child of a fighter pilot and a professional worrier. At birth, I was assigned National ID #0001688816681. It’s a prime number, with no multiplicative factors. You may notice the auspicious sequence “888” in the middle of my ID number. A statistical improbability, to be sure. But every ID taped to the Wall of 360-Degree Feedbacks is full of statistical improbabilities.

Brenda says I should control my rage, make small talk, tell jokes.

Like a normal person.

Here’s a joke. One mathematician says to another, “My ID number is 000137439855.” The other mathematician says, “Crazy! What are the odds?”

That’s the whole joke.

You may also notice that my ID number 0001688816681 contains three leading zeroes. The idea there is to accommodate the trillion future Chinese citizens who will populate our space colonies.

3. The ring was a gift

Oslo, Norway, two years later.

I am an instructor at the Kolmogorov Institute of Mathematics at the University of Oslo.

The name on my office door is fake, because I’m in hiding, although I can’t remember my real name or even the reason I’m hiding. My face in the mirror is an Asian one, so I lie to my colleagues and say that I come from the Soviet East. Fortunately, few people here in Norway ask questions, especially in winter.

My memories may be gone, but my mathematical faculties are intact. I have just completed a private proof of the Riemann Hypothesis, a classic problem in prime number theory, unsolved since 1859. The more I work on the proof, the simpler it gets, which is always a good sign.

I check my mood ring. Bluish-green: I am relaxed and aware of my surroundings.

This ring was a gift, I think.

I open my math notebook and write down that fact.

Given by: Unknown.

Date: Unknown.

Place: Unknown.

4. Spreading false corporate gossip

Peking, 1972.

Cherry Xu, my HR representative at Alicent Technologies, scheduled a struggle session for two o’clock. She’d been impelled no doubt by my recent outburst at the Wall of 360-Degree Feedbacks. I’d had two such “incidents” before, and as she put it, one more will make three, an obvious mathematical fact.

Brenda says I take things too literally.

Beeps erupted from two computer terminals, mine and Sunny Li’s. We pushed our chairs back and walked down the row of engineers to Cherry Xu’s office.

Sunny Li and I had been friends since the day we both arrived at Alicent. We had dinner together regularly, and she always looked out for me. Sunny Li was a project manager at the company. Or a product manager. I could never remember the difference.

Division head Krystal Zhang was already there, standing and talking with Cherry Xu. Also present were two other classmates, introduced as Carrie Hu and Edsger Wang. At Alicent, we were supposed to say “classmate” instead of “co-worker,” because of the nice, casual feeling it gave.

Only important people like Cherry Xu had offices at Alicent; the rest of us sat shoulder-to-shoulder up and down our rows of terminals. Cherry Xu’s austere office gave the impression that what little time she might have to decorate it was consistently sucked up by having to deal with the problems caused by us. Her desk terminal had a detached keyboard, instead of the usual built-in one, and its screen was covered by some kind of polarized film that prevented viewing at an angle. Her office walls were bare except for a framed color photograph of our company’s Chairman and CEO.

After we assembled, Cherry Xu asked us to push the conference table to one wall and roll six office chairs into a circle. I looked at the faces around the room. No one was older than twenty-one, with the possible exception of Cherry Xu.

“Let’s start with the fact that we’re all lucky to be here,” Cherry Xu began, “given the ongoing semi-permanent disruptions in the labor market, and so forth.”

She pressed a button on her Strollman cassette player to start recording the meeting. The empty audiocassette box on her desk advertised “60 minutes recording time,” which I optimistically took as an upper bound on how long the struggle session would last; at the end, the audiotape would be uploaded to the mainframe computer for speech recognition and persistent storage.

I looked past Cherry Xu at the Chairman’s framed photo on the wall. Studying it, I thought I detected a faint smirk on his face. Had that smirk been secretly added by some low-level artist in Alicent’s Office of Corporate Identity? I imagined the hapless illustrator attending her own struggle session right now.

“Krystal Zhang is the senior manager in this meeting,” continued Cherry Xu, “so I will consult with her about the outcome. I mean, she’ll make the decision.” Cherry Xu hesitated then, as if trying to remember something but ultimately failing.

Krystal arrived to Alicent Technologies the same day as me and Sunny Li. The three of us were assigned to the same lunch table on that first day, and because we were all strangers in our cohort, we three decided to stick together. During the first weeks of orientation, Krystal Zhang struggled a lot, unable to master the basic technical materials. I covered for her and answered her questions, but Sunny Li wasn’t as patient, finally decreeing that we couldn’t be friends with Krystal anymore. Later, Krystal became a manager, which made sense, because everyone knows that managers don’t need to know all the technical details.

“What’s this about?” Sunny Li demanded. She was shifting around in her seat, apparently unable to find a comfortable position. While she searched underneath her chair for the height adjustment knob, Cherry Xu answered, in a pretty serious tone, that Sunny Li had been accused of spreading false corporate gossip. She said we were to address the topic with innovation, agility, passion, and so forth, following the Chairman’s procedure. With a nervous laugh, she admitted that “procedure” was probably not the right word, given that the Chairman was famously against them. Corporate procedures are the dead hand of the past, the Chairman wrote in the Alicent employee handbook, trying to maintain its grip on the living.

“This is bullshit,” Sunny Li declared.

5. I move to postpone or whatever

Suddenly, I realized this wasn’t my struggle session.

It was Sunny Li’s.

By now, everybody knew somebody who’d been kicked out of the company. Such people were inevitably unable to get another job in tech. The companies shared a surprising amount of data with each other: onboarding dates, graduation notices, performance evaluations, personal identifying information. Rumor had it that one classmate did get a job at another tech company, but it turned out the job was to water their indoor plants, which didn’t cover his rent, so he had to move.

Cherry Xu didn’t reply to Sunny Li.

Instead, she took out a stack of pre-filled index cards and handed them to the boy on her right.

“Should I take the card on the top?” the boy Edsger Wang asked.

“It doesn’t matter,” Cherry Xu said.

“Or take one from the middle?”

“It doesn’t matter.”

As this was happening, Sunny Li pulled out her orange, pocket-sized Alicent employee handbook and started desperately waving it around while tapping the cover illustration with her finger. On the cover of the latest handbook, a cartoon cricket stands surveying the ocean from the top of a cliff.

“The Director of Corporate Identity approved this drawing!” said Sunny Li. “It’s saying, you know, implying, that the Chairman is leading our company over the edge of a cliff. I’m not spreading corporate gossip. You can see for yourself, right here!”

Then Sunny Li started frantically flipping the pages of the booklet. “The handbook has a section about how corporate logos get approved --”

Struggling to find the right page, she suddenly lost momentum.

I couldn’t follow exactly what Sunny Li was saying, and I thought the others might feel the same. I wanted to help her -- in meetings like these, it was important to say to the right thing in the right way.

“Sunny Li,” I said, “I think I get it, but could you put it in a different --”

Carrie Hu interrupted, “Are we talking about employee handbook section 9.16?”

Sunny Li looked up from her handbook, stricken.

“Because section 9.16 isn’t really relevant to this situation,” Carrie Hu continued.

“What?” said Sunny Li.

Krystal Zhang asked Carrie Hu to quote a more relevant section, which Carrie Hu did, from memory. During the re-organization, people who could quote passages from memory earned high status, so everyone tried to do it.

“I suggest,” said Krystal Zhang, “that we evaluate Carrie Hu’s passage in an agile, innovative way.”

Cherry Xu cleared her throat. “And let me clarify, at this point, that either Sunny Li or the Director of Corporate Identity will be graduating today, depending on the outcome of this struggle session.”

“The guy’s not even here!” wailed Sunny Li, looking around wildly. “I move to postpone, or whatever!”

6. This meeting is over

Sunny Li swept her gaze around the room before fixing it on me.

“I move to postpone,” she shouted, “because first we need to deal with the case of Vivian Wu!”

“I’m sorry?” said Krystal Zhang.

“Vivian Wu,” Sunny Li said, struggling to quiet her voice. “Vivian Wu slept with a Level-D2 Contractor from the Ministry of Defense.”

I squeezed the armrests of my chair and counted the seconds of silence. One, two, three, four seconds of everyone looking at me. To avert their gaze, I checked my mood ring: Red.

“That’s right,” Sunny Li said. “Vivian Wu slept with Level-D2 contractor Gerald Yang in exchange for military secrets. Then she passed those secrets to a female foreign agent that she frequently consorts with.”

“What do you mean, consorts with?” Krystal asked.

“Consorts with!” Sunny Li said loudly.

She was starting to lose her grip again.

“Sleeps with!”

An awkward silence followed.

Cherry Xu finally suggested that this might be a matter for the police.

“Correct,” said Carrie Hu, flipping through the pages of her orange handbook. “Although we should recognize that fraternization with third-party clients is definitely prohibited in cases of conflict of interest. And it’s a separate violation if an intimate relationship hasn’t been disclosed within one week of … you know, within a week of the potential infraction.“

Edsger Wang raised his hand.

“What?” snapped Cherry Xu.

“What you call a person who’s attracted to both men and women?”

He was thumbing through the index of his orange booklet, failing to find whatever word he was looking for. Anyone not already holding their own copy of the handbook immediately dove for it … except for me.

Instead, I rocked back and forth in my chair, not knowing what to do.

Brenda said to count.

I leaned back and started to count the ceiling tiles, left-to-right, then right-to-left.

Sunny Li’s accusations weren’t true, but they were true enough. Once HR started digging, things would come to light and I’d be fired.

One hundred sixty-eight ceiling tiles in all.

7. A cesspool of drugs, explosions, and anarchy

I had two problems.

One was how to get out of the current mess, right then and there, with all these people, in Cherry Xu’s office. For that, I had the Projector, though I didn’t want to use it. It wasn’t good to use the Projector, and besides, it didn’t always work. And besides: I’d vowed not to use it on friends and classmates. And besides: the side effects.

The other problem was so what? My job at Alicent was terrible, but I didn’t have a plan for life outside of it. I had a dream, of course, which was to move to California and live with my boyfriend, a mathematician named Carson Hawke, who’d exchanged Strollnet audio messages with me. I won’t tell you that Carson Hawke invented the Strollnet, or at least the cryptographic protocols for it, because then really you wouldn’t believe me. You’d say he was imaginary.

But he’s not.

He sent me a mixtape, which I could play for you.

The reason it was a dream was that California was a cesspool of drugs, explosions, and anarchy, where the people’s chosen Governor was a woman who made the kinds of films I don’t know the English word for. Even if the lawless breakaway region didn’t require a passport to get into, I still needed a passport to leave China, and the Ministry of Defense wasn’t giving them out.

Brenda could get me out, or at least that what she’d said one night, high on white stimulant powder. But these days, Brenda was nowhere to be found.

8. But sometimes

My hands were shaking.

First things first, Sunny Li was no longer a friend, and Cherry Xu was certainly no friend either.

I held my breath and projected a thought into Cherry Xu’s head: This meeting is over.

“This meeting is over,” she said.

“What?” said Krystal Zhang.

“What?” said Sunny Li.

You have another meeting, I projected. You’re not feeling well.

“I’m not feeling well,” Cherry Xu said.

Around the circle of swivel chairs, the mouths of the participants dropped open. In the silence that followed, I eased myself from my seat and left the room.

Back when I thought Brenda believed in me, and wanted the best for me, she showed me how to control my extra-sensory action ability, what I came to call my “Projector.” Only then did I realize that I’d been accidentally projecting my whole life -- accidentally manipulating people, Brenda said -- instead of developing normal social skills.

At least I learned to turn it on and off.

It’s bad to turn it on, according to the classified literature Brenda gave me, because it creates “cognitive dissonance” in the “target person,” which means they struggle to fit the new, projected belief into the network of their other beliefs. I create an “elevated mental load” that’s untraceable, but the target person’s brain eventually associates me with some vague form of unpleasantness, and they learn to avoid me. I guess you could say that stunted social skills and a lack of friends are side effects of the Projector, although, according to Brenda’s material, the chemical side effects are much more serious.

Anyway, it’s better not to use the Projector, especially on friends and classmates.

But sometimes you’re in a jam.

9. Thank you for the opportunity

I walked from Cherry Xu’s office to the Wall of 360-Degree Feedbacks and pinned my last note.

 

Dear all,

This is my resignation letter.

It has not been a pleasure working here, but

I have learned a lot, and thank you for the opportunity.

Sunny Li can go fuck herself.

Vivian Wu

 

My hand shook as I wrote the last line, but I wasn’t sorry. She’d turned on me, and no matter where things went from here, it was only a matter of time before Alicent got rid of me, and then I’d be on the street with all the other graduates, unemployed and unemployable.

Next, I went to the mainframe computer room, my late-night haven. It was daytime now, so the room’s only occupant was a lone systems programmer wearing Strollman headphones and typing on his terminal.

I walked to the peripherals area, where, pinned to a corkboard was an oversized color photo of a naked woman, a twice-creased page tacked up since long before I joined the company.

Miss April 1967, Satomi Nakamura.

The photograph was unlikely yet vital evidence against me, something that could tie me to an anonymous piece of software I’d written during my late-night sessions. I unpinned the photo and folded it into the back pocket of my jeans. At the door of the mainframe room, I turned and said, “Goodbye.” The systems programmer didn’t look up -- maybe he didn’t hear me, or maybe he guessed, correctly, that I was saying goodbye to the room rather than him.

I took the elevator down to Alicent’s vast front lobby. On the wall behind security, a huge video screen projected a canned, year-by-year history of the company. I knew the story by heart, having eventually viewed every slide while waiting for the morning face-scanner. The current image was the last one in the sequence:

 

1972. A year of reflection and rebirth in which Alicent, with the teams’ full backing of the business, accelerated the development of our products, increased operational efficiency, and created greater business value.

 

As I walked past, the giant screen flipped back to the year of our Chairman’s birth, showing the dirt streets of Peking only one generation ago. No gleaming skyscrapers, no tech campuses, just a picture of how you’d imagine the poorest city in the poorest country on Earth.

I wondered what I would do if I could turn back time. Would I, like Alicent Technologies, admit no mistakes and forge the same path, over and over? It was natural, I guessed, to feel philosophical after the day’s events, and wonder if I’d done things correctly.

I wondered if I was actually living in the best of all possible worlds.

But I wasn’t.

The moment the face-scanner allowed me to leave the building, a policeman grabbed my upper arm. No more cat-and-mouse. Somewhere deep in the vast Ministry of Defense complex, Level-D2 contractor Gerald Yang was smiling. He’d found his man. Only his “man” was me.
